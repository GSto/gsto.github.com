How to Bill Daily or Weekly

Feb 21st, 2014

When it comes to freelancing, you may think you only have two options when it comes to how you bill your clients: You can bill by the hour, or you can bill by the project. But these aren’t your only options, and in many cases they are not your best ones.

Two alternatives to this is billing by the day, or billing by the week. These billing structures are a little more uncommon, but in my experience as a consultant, they have been the most effective and productive. Since these structures are a little bit different, they often require a little bit of explanation.

What’s In A Day?

An hour is a definite measure of time. it’s 60 minutes. When you bill a client for a hour, they expect to be providing value during those 60 minutes. A day is also a definite measure of time, but if you bill a client for a day they don’t expect 24 hours of productivity from you.

You make think that a work day of 8 hours is a more reasonable assumption, but that also isn’t the case. Many studies have shown that creative workers are only productive for 5 to 6 hours a day, and that increased hours can in fact lead to decreased productivity. So when it comes to billing by the day, it just means whatever you think a good day’s work means to you. It doesn’t mean that it has to be all of your time, or that if you bill for a day you can’t do any other productive work for another client.

If you have been working in freelancing or consulting and have been keeping track of your hours, you can use that data to determine your day rate.

Weekly Billing and Deadlines

Weekly billing tends to work a little bit differently. When you bill by the week, you do so with a goal and a deliverable for the week. Weekly billing is in a way like billing a flat project rate, but you are delivering smaller projects in a smaller time frame.

Weekly billing may seem strange at first, but if you think about it, this is how salaried employees basically work.

With my clients, I set milestones every two weeks, and tell them that I will deliver on those milestones in the time frame. It doesn’t matter if I am working 30 hours a week, or 40, or 60, or 5. As long as the results and value are delivered, that is what counts.

The other thing I like about weekly billing is that it makes scheduling my availability much simpler. I can look at my calendar, see that I have worked booked for the next 6 weeks, and have a clear date for when I am available for more work.

How to Decide on a Pricing Model

I don’t think that any consultant should stick to just one pricing structure, unless you work on a narrow type of project. Different projects and different clients will work better with different billing structures. Here is when I would consider each one:

  • Hourly Billing: For jobs where the scope is unknown and flexibility is needed.
  • Flat Rate Billing: When the scope of a project can be well defined and the project is small.
  • Daily Billing: Works well for one day consulting arrangements, and also for projects that you want to commit to part time (such as 2 - 3 days per week)
  • Weekly Billing: My personal favorite. Tends to work well on larger projects, and projects that need flexibility and aren’t committed to a hard scope.

By thinking about how you price and position your services, you can come up with plans that can make your scheduling better, and help you price your services more aggressively.
